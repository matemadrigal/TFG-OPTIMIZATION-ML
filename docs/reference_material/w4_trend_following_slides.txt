Dynamic Trading Strategies

A dynamic strategy is defined as a set of dynamically updated
weights, also called the signal st-1 multiplied by excess returns rt,
i.e., the strategy returns

                                       rst = st-1rt

or, if we track final wealth

WT = T rts = T st-1rt

                            t=1  t=1

The concept of strategy is used frequently in quantitative finance, in
particular, self-financing strategies, see, e.g., Duffie, Dynamic Asset
Pricing Theory, p 83ff.

Trend Following
Trend-Following

     Momentum or Trend Following

         · Most widely known Algo style
         · Ben Graham (the father of value investing wrote about it (and

           invested in it)
         · Proven to have worked for at least 200 years.

         · Two major forms:
                 · Cross-sectional, i.e., a Momentum factor
                 · Time-series, e.g., a CTA

         · Underlies almost all the CTA industry
         · Not a diversified source of return
         · Often thought of as an insurance trade (significant downside

           protection)

Defining Momentum

     Definition (A version of Momentum)
     In it's most basic form: Find a moving average/trend.

         · Go Long if
                 · Level > Moving average (+threshold)
                 · Short Moving Average > Long Moving Average
                 · Trend is going up
                 · Some combination

         · Go Short if
                 · level < Moving average (- threshold)
                 · Short Moving Average < Long Moving Average
                 · Trend is going down
                 · Some combination

Momentum Factoids

Momentum's desirable properties

   · It tends to perform when vol is high. (counter: when vol is low it
      tends to underperform)

   · It tends to have positively skewed returns (i.e., it may not win
      often, but when it wins, it wins big)

   · The skewness of returns appears greater when measured over
      longer horizons (e.g., when investors look at quarterly returns
      they are very highly skewed, compared to daily returns).

   · Its payoff is similar to that of a straddle, at least empirically.
      (Fung-Hsieh analysed this despite it being a heuristic )

   · Makes money in trending markets, including down-trends
      (insurance)

   · Loses money in sideways markets (pays for insurance)

We investigate the skewness over different horizons and We also

investigate responsiveness to changes in trend.                          15

Fascinating skewness

     Why is skewness so interesting?

         · Even an un-skewed return distribution can lead to positive
           skewness

         · (Almost all) risk-averse investors prefer:
                 · more positive returns
                 · less volatility
                 · more positive skewness

         · Why is skewness so strange?
                 · Interaction between future signal (at time T + k) involving terms
                   from T to T + k - 1
                 · Interaction between future returns (at times T to T + k - 1)

         · In reality mean, standard deviation and Sharpe also (even more)
           interesting!

Momentum and Responsiveness
Responsiveness

         · Short-term MA -
                 · more responsive - captures established trends near their start
                 · higher transaction costs - bleeds away in a sideways market

         · Long-term MA
                 · less responsive - lagged response to new turning points
                 · lower transaction costs - doesn't lose money in a sideways market

Rolling Futures

     Rolling Futures

         · Futures must be rolled to avoid cash settlement/physical
           settlement

         · Roll dates based on either:
                 · Min(last trade date, first notice date, first delivery date) ­ k days
                 · When the volume of the 2nd contract exceed than on the 1st
                   contract

         · Need the key dates and volumes for first 2 contracts
         · Can roll some futures sporadically rather than deterministically.
         · Roll entire chain at same time ­ 1st, 2nd, etc, out to Nth contract.
         · Rolling incurs one-sided transaction costs.
         · Continuous Futures Contracts for Backtesting
         · Continuous Futures Methodology for Backtesting
         · Cont Futures for Backtesting and Technical Analysis

Updating Futures Data

     Storing Futures Data
        - Periodic bulk download of all the timeseries needed OR use a
           real-time downloader which updates all series on fixed
           schedule.
        - Make sure full history of expiries are available (for proper
           rolling).
        - Bulk download missing series etc.
        - Pull in reference index if available (for carry calcs, signal)
        - Convert all to at least 2-digit year expiry (if not 4-digit)
        - Store in a suitable database.
        - Must store unadjusted futures prices (for levels and carry calcs)

An Exercise
Exercise

     Background - Momentum in Equities
         · Recall - Equities Indices typically trend in the medium term (e.g.,
           1y), but mean-revert in the short term (e.g., 10 days)
         · Carhart recognised this and created the momentum factor
           sorting returns from 12m less the previous month.
         · At that point buy the winners and hold.
         · It is also known that momentum stops working after about 18m.
           Sometimes this is called mean-reversion even though the term
           is loose. (It is also unclear if we need to worry about this)
